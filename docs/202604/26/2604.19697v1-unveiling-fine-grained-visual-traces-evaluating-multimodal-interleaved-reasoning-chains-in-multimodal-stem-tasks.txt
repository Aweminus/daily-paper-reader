Abstract 

Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have shown promising reasoning abilities, yet evaluating their performance in specialized do-mains remains challenging. STEM reasoning is a particularly valuable testbed because it provides highly verifiable feedback, but exist-ing benchmarks often permit unimodal short-cuts due to modality redundancy and focus mainly on final-answer accuracy, overlook-ing the reasoning process itself. To address this challenge, we introduce STEP STEM : a graduate-level benchmark of 283 problems across mathematics, physics, chemistry, bi-ology, and engineering for fine-grained eval-uation of cross-modal reasoning in MLLMs. 

STEP STEM is constructed through a rigorous curation pipeline that enforces strict comple-mentarity between textual and visual inputs. We further propose a general step-level eval-uation framework for both text-only chain-of-thought and interleaved image-text reasoning, using dynamic programming to align predicted reasoning steps with multiple reference solu-tions. Experiments across a wide range of models show that current MLLMs still rely heavily on textual reasoning, with even Gem-ini 3.1 Pro and Claude Opus 4.6 achieving only 38.29% accuracy. These results highlight substantial headroom for genuine cross-modal STEM reasoning and position STEP STEM as a benchmark for fine-grained evaluation of mul-timodal reasoning. Source code is available at 

https://github.com/lll-hhh/STEPSTEM .

1 Introduction 

Recent advances in Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) (Wang et al., 2025; Qwen Team, 2026; Deng et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025; Cui et al., 2025; Team et al., 2026) have substantially expanded the scope of multimedia information pro-cessing. In addition to understand increasingly

complex visual and textual inputs, MLLMs are capable of generating intermediate reasoning tra-jectories in text, image, or interleaved image-text formats, and derive the final answer based on these trajectories (Su et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025a; Chern et al., 2025). This emerging capability has made MLLMs a promising foundation for solving com-plex multimodal reasoning problems. Among exist-ing multimodal reasoning scenarios, STEM tasks are particularly attractive because of their strong verifiability: the correctness of the final answer is often objectively checkable, which provides a natural basis for supervising and analyzing the rea-soning process itself. Despite the growing attention to multimodal STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematical) reasoning, the field still faces sev-eral important challenges. (1) First, current MLLM reasoning often exhibit a form of modality collapse ,in which they over-rely on textual cues while under-utilizing visual evidence, leading to weak visual grounding and degraded performance on tasks that require genuine visual reasoning (Zhao et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025b; Yao et al., 2025). In STEM rea-soning, the modality collapse becomes more sig-nificant because of the detailed textual description on STEM benchmarks. (2) Second, the genera-tion and understanding of structured STEM visu-als remain limited. Prior work shows that scien-tific figures, diagrams, and plots are poorly cov-ered by existing training data, while current image-generation pipelines are largely optimized for nat-uralistic imagery rather than structured, logically constrained visuals with precise layouts, symbols, and labels (Li et al., 2024b; Wei et al., 2025). (3) Third, current evaluation and post-training prac-tices still emphasize final-answer correctness more than intermediate-step quality. The broader reason-ing literature has shown that chain-of-thought ratio-nales are not always faithful to the model’s actual reasoning process, while both LLM and MLLM

studies suggest that step-wise supervision can bet-ter support reliable reasoning than outcome-only signals (Turpin et al., 2023; Lightman et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025b). As a result, it remains diffi-cult to determine whether a model truly performs grounded cross-modal reasoning or merely reaches the correct answer through superficial shortcuts. To address these limitations, we introduce STEP -STEM , a new multimodal STEM benchmark with fine-grained reasoning step evaluation that includes engineering, physics, mathematics, chemistry, and biology subset. STEP STEM contains graduate-level multimodal STEM problems that require both textual information and visual evidence to derive the correct solution, thereby discouraging unimodal shortcuts and encouraging genuine cross-modal rea-soning. As illustrated in Figure 1, during data con-struction, we deliberately curate problems whose solutions cannot be recovered from text alone or vi-sion alone. More importantly, for each problem we provide one or more ground-truth solution trajec-tories that contain both textual intermediate steps and visual intermediate steps. Built upon S TEP STEM, we further introduce a step-level evaluation framework for multimodal reasoning. Instead of evaluating only the final an-swer, our framework first measures the similarity between each predicted reasoning step and the an-notated ground-truth solution steps, and then aligns the predicted trajectory with one or multiple ref-erence ground truth trajectories through dynamic programming to obtain an overall step-level rea-soning score. In this way, the proposed protocol captures not only whether a model reaches the cor-rect answer, but also whether it follows a plausible, grounded, and interpretable multimodal reasoning path. We evaluate a broad range of representative MLLMs on S TEP STEM. Experimental results show that S TEP STEM remains highly challeng-ing for existing models, revealing substantial room for improvement in genuine multimodal STEM rea-soning. Moreover, the proposed step-level scores provide a much more fine-grained view of model behavior than answer-only evaluation, enabling us to identify differences in reasoning quality, modal-ity usage, and grounding ability that are otherwise obscured by final accuracy alone. In summary, our contributions are three-fold: 

(1) A new multimodal STEM reasoning bench-mark that requires cross-modality reasoning :We introduce STEP STEM , consisting of 283 care-fully curated problems that enforce strict comple-mentarity to prevent unimodal shortcuts. 

(2) A fine-grained evaluation framework for in-termediate reasoning step evaluation : We pro-pose a step-level evaluation framework for sys-tematically assessing intermediate multimodal rea-soning steps beyond final-answer accuracy. 

(3) A comprehensive empirical analysis : We eval-uate representative MLLMs on STEP STEM and provide empirical insights into their capabilities and limitations in cross-modal STEM reasoning. We further show that step-level scores strongly cor-relate with final-answer accuracy, highlighting the importance of evaluating reasoning processes in addition to final outcomes. 

2 Related Works 

2.1 Multimodal Chain-of-Thought Reasoning 

Traditional Chain-of-Thought (CoT) techniques have achieved remarkable success in pure text log-ical deduction; however, when confronted with complex scenarios highly dependent on spatial per-ception and geometric intuition, pure text deriva-tions often lack cognitively meaningful physical an-chors. Consequently, multimodal interleaved CoT has emerged, with the core objective of decoupling the complex problem-solving process into a series of intermediate steps where textual deduction and visual evidence are deeply interwoven. Current explicit multimodal CoT methods pri-marily evolve along two technical trajectories. The first entails tool-augmented designs (Li et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2025a; Hu et al., 2024; Duan et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2026; Li et al., 2025d), which leverage external visual modules or code interpreters to assist reasoning; nevertheless, the interleaving process of such methods often exhibits high fragility and low flexibility. The second tra-jectory focuses on intrinsic visual CoT (Intrinsic VCoT) under unified model architectures. For in-stance, THINKMORPH (Gu et al., 2025) reveals advanced reasoning characteristics that emerge un-der the multimodal interleaved training paradigm, while MathCanvas (Shi et al., 2025a) constructs an intrinsic VCoT framework specifically designed for mathematical reasoning. However, existing main-stream text-to-image models are predominantly pre-trained on general open-domain scenes, such as natural landscapes, daily scenarios, and virtual characters. 2.2 Multimodal Reasoning Dataset 

In parallel with the limitations of current genera-tion models, another critical challenge lies in the scarcity of high-quality multimodal reasoning data tailored for downstream tasks. Early efforts in mul-timodal reasoning primarily focus on visual under-standing and perception, where the core objective is to interpret visual inputs and map them into tex-tual answers. Representative benchmarks include MMMU (Yue et al., 2024), MathVista (Lu et al., 2023), ScienceQA (Lu et al., 2022), and Math-Verse (Zhang et al., 2024). While these datasets have significantly advanced the evaluation of mul-timodal models, their solution processes are pre-dominantly text-centric. Unified benchmarks like Uni-MMMU (Zou et al., 2025), RealUnify (Shi et al., 2025b) and others (Wang et al., 2026; Huang et al., 2026) evaluate both perception and genera-tion but typically focus on foundational tasks. They lack the graduate-level depth and systematic proto-cols required to audit intermediate reasoning steps, leaving the logical coherence of cross-modal pro-cesses unverified. Recent work has explored multimodal reason-ing with visual generation, such as Zebra-CoT (Li et al., 2025a) and THINKMORPH (Gu et al., 2025), which emphasize reasoning within the vi-sual modality. However, existing STEM bench-marks still suffer from modality redundancy : mod-els can often rely on textual shortcuts without gen-uinely using visual evidence. In addition, many current datasets are not sufficiently challenging to rigorously evaluate state-of-the-art models on deep, multi-step reasoning. 

2.3 Fine-Grained Evaluation and Supervision of Reasoning Processes 

Traditional evaluation protocols overly rely on final-answer accuracy, thereby neglecting the faithful-ness and logical coherence of the reasoning path. To overcome this limitation, the academic commu-nity is accelerating the transition toward a process-oriented monitoring paradigm. 3.1 Overview 

Our S TEP STEM consists of two core components: the rigorous curation of a multimodal dataset (de-tailed in Section 3.2) and a fine-grained evaluation protocol: (1) For the dataset, we carefully choose 283 graduate-level multimodal STEM reasoning problems and provide one or more ground truth reference solutions. The overall dataset construc-tion workflow is illustrated in Figure 1, and the domain distributions of S TEP STEM is shown in Figure 2. (2) For the evaluation phase, our frame-work assesses both final-answer correctness and intermediate reasoning quality. 3.2 Data Curation 

To construct a multimodal STEM benchmark tai-lored for process-level evaluation, we specifically curate two categories of problems characterized by substantial visual dependency: (1) strongly depen-dent problems, where reasoning cannot proceed reliably without the accompanying images, and (2) 

reasoning-simplifying problems, where visual in-formation significantly compresses the reasoning chain and reduces semantic ambiguity. The overall curation pipeline comprises data collection, data filtering, and solution structuring. This rigorous process was conducted by eight experts with in-terdisciplinary backgrounds, who transformed raw multimodal data into structured reasoning chains Figure 3: Illustration of fine-grained evaluation protocol for S TEP STEM. 

suitable for fine-grained process evaluation. The detailed statistical distributions of the dataset are presented in Table 1. 

3.2.1 Data Collection 

During the collection phase, experts gathered poten-tial samples from public competitions, undergradu-ate textbooks, and other open educational resources. For each candidate problem, we recorded the prob-lem statement, reference image, ground-truth an-swer, and a standard solution trajectory detailing key intermediate steps. We adopted a task-attribute-first principle to assess visual dependency: prob-lems were retained only if their resolution strictly required the geometric structure, spatial relations, or local details of the image, or if the image explic-itly shortened the reasoning path. Notably, to ele-vate the evaluation dimensionality, we specifically included 14 drawing tasks where the final output is required to be an image . This inclusion fun-damentally extends the evaluation paradigm from standard “image-text understanding to text gener-ation” to the more challenging “iage-text under-standing to image generation”. 

3.2.2 Data Filtering 

To ensure data quality and evaluation operability, we established rigorous exclusion criteria. We dis-carded samples where: (1) the image is purely dec-orative or highly redundant with the text; (2) the problem is ambiguous or solely tests OCR capabil-ities; or (3) the image quality is too low to support stable annotation. Furthermore, we implemented an objective two-ended truncation filter for problem difficulty. We removed “overly simple” problems that experts universally agreed required no explicit intermediate reasoning, thereby preventing the task from degrading into basic visual recognition. Con-versely, we excluded “overly difficult” problems where experts could not reach a stable consensus or formulate a verifiable step-level standard process. This dual filtering ensures that the retained sam-ples maintain high consistency in both verifiable reasoning and moderate difficulty. It is worth noting that the distribution of test in-stances varies across domains, reflecting the vary-ing degrees of intrinsic visual dependency across scientific disciplines. To prevent unimodal short-cuts, we exclusively retained problems where the solution strictly necessitates the interpretation of spatial relations or geometric structures. Engineer-ing and Physics naturally offer a higher density of such visually-indispensable tasks at the graduate level , whereas many candidate problems in Mathe-matics or Biology were excluded for being solvable via pure textual logic. 

3.2.3 Solution Structuring 

Following the filtering phase, the expert-verified reference solutions were unified into an interleaved image-text step structure suitable for multimodal evaluation. To enhance structural efficiency and standardization, we utilized GPT-5.4 Codex with fixed prompts for the initial step-level decomposi-tion and modality tag assignment. It is important to note that GPT-5.4 Codex was strictly limited to formatting the expert-provided reference solutions and was not used to independently generate new answers. This automated structuralization was fol-lowed by rigorous manual review and correction to ensure each step constitutes an independent and verifiable reasoning unit. For visual steps, experts further annotated the local visual evidence supporting the current rea-soning utilizing bounding boxes (BBoxes). When a single step relied on multiple dispersed regions, multiple BBoxes were permitted. This design al-lows visual steps to pinpoint the specific local ev-idence actively participating in the reasoning pro-cess, providing fine-grained supervision for subse-quent step-level image alignment evaluation. Additionally, to construct alternative valid rea-soning paths (Solution 2 and Solution 3) for sam-ples exhibiting multi-path solution characteristics, we introduced a combined mechanism of expert manual exploration and LLM-inspired gener-ation . Specifically, experts actively explored di-verse problem-solving strategies based on their do-main backgrounds. Simultaneously, we input the problems into advanced multimodal models such as Gemini 3.1 Pro and Claude Opus 4.6 to detect whether there were significantly different solution strategies from the primary reference solution. For novel solutions independently proposed by experts, as well as those generated by models that were rigorously reviewed by experts to be logically con-sistent, correct, and substantially different, we stan-dardized them into additional alternative reference solutions. Therefore, these multiple solutions rep-resent distinct and valid deductive paths rather than mere linguistic paraphrases, providing critical sup-port for the subsequent best-over-solutions evalua-tion. Through this comprehensive pipeline, we ulti-mately constructed a dataset comprising 283 prob-lems across six domains: Engineering, Physics, Chemistry, Biology, Mathematics, and Other. 

3.3 Evaluation Protocol 

To rigorously assess the quality of multimodal reasoning, we propose a fine-grained evaluation framework that decouples the interleaved reason-ing traces into explicit local step-level alignment and implicit global logic coverage. For textual steps, we further annotate key points to support fine-grained evaluation of semantic correctness and reasoning completeness, while the evaluation em-phasizes semantic consistency with the reference solution as well as coverage of annotated key points. For visual steps, we annotate one or more important bounding boxes to specify the regions that should be attended to or referenced by the model at each stage. To establish a unified cross-modal computation, we first define a shared notation system: let GT and 

GI denote the sets of ground-truth (GT) textual and visual steps, respectively; similarly, let PT and PI

denote the predicted textual and visual steps. The complete protocol consists of modality-specific lo-cal alignments, large language model (LLM) based global judgements, and a final metric fusion mecha-nism designed to handle problem-solving diversity.

3.3.2 Image Reasoning Steps Evaluation 

For visual reasoning steps, such as intermediate geometric plots or state diagrams, we employ a Many-to-Many (M2M) alignment strategy based on deep visual features. This strategy maintains strict mathematical symmetry with the textual to-ken alignment. We utilize a visual encoder ϕ(·) to extract fea-ture maps. 3.3.3 Global Logic Coverage Evaluation 

While local step-level alignment captures explicit step-by-step logic, models may successfully arrive at correct deductions via valid but unstructured alternative paths. To accommodate this flexibility, we introduce an LLM-based global judge to assess whole-trace coverage. This evaluation operates from a macro perspec-tive. The entire raw predicted trajectory is fed into the judge alongside the GT reference points, encompassing both textual key points and visual regions. Acting as a generous evaluator, the judge determines whether each critical GT proposition is semantically entailed or mathematically repre-sented in the candidate’s trajectory. It outputs a binary decision of 1 for covered and 0 for miss-ing for each reference point. The global coverage score Sglobal is then calculated as the average of these binary decisions across all references.

Table 2 summarizes the overall performance on 269 problems with textual final answers and 14 prob-lems with image-based final answers. The benchmark is highly challenging. Overall, the results indicate that our benchmark remains dif-ficult even for the strongest contemporary MLLMs. On the 269 problems with textual final answers, the best accuracy is only 38.29%, achieved by Gemini 3.1 Pro and Claude Opus 4.6. GPT-5.4 + GPT-Image achieves the best Fused score (71.52) and the highest Global score (80.94), while Gemini 2.5 Flash Image attains the best Img. Acc. (50.80%) and a strong Local-I score (64.10). Nevertheless, their final-answer accuracy remains limited, especially on the text-answer subset, where GPT-5.4 + GPT-Image reaches 33.21% and Gemini 2.5 Flash Image reaches only 15.61%. These find-ings suggest that, although current unified models are capable of generating plausible intermediate visual structures, they still struggle to fully exploit the generated visual information to solve challeng-ing STEM reasoning problems. 

Interleaved generation alone is insufficient for effective multimodal reasoning. Interleaved generation models obtain non-trivial scores on image-related process metrics, indicating a lim-ited ability to produce intermediate visual content. For example, OmniGen2 achieves 59.05 on Local-I, and Bagel reaches 48.70 on Global-I. However, both fail to generate correct image-based final an-swers, yielding 0% accuracy on the image-answer subset. This suggests that interleaving image gener-ation into the reasoning chain alone is insufficient. Effective multimodal STEM reasoning requires not only generating intermediate visual evidence, but also integrating it coherently into the reasoning process to arrive at correct final solutions. 

4.3 Analysis and Case Study 4.3.1 Analysis of Step-Level Evaluation Scores Step-level process scores are strongly associated with final-answer correctness. As shown by the blue intervals in Figure 4, trajectories that lead to correct final answers consistently receive higher fused process scores than those ending with in-correct answers. This pattern is further confirmed by the correctness split in Table 3. For example, the fused score increases from 64.73 to 81.43 for Gemini 2.5 Flash, from 68.81 to 78.41 for GPT-5.4 + GPT-Image, from 47.46 to 65.84 for Bagel, and from 35.33 to 42.86 for Gemini 3.1 Pro when moving from incorrect to correct cases. The con-sistency of this trend across unified models, inter-leaved generation models, and understanding-only models suggests that the proposed process score captures meaningful variation in reasoning quality rather than incidental overlap with the reference solutions. 

Higher-performing models tend to obtain higher process scores. Beyond the correctness split, we also observe that models with stronger multimodal reasoning ability generally achieve higher abso-lute process scores. This result highlights an important ad-vantage of step-level evaluation: it can distinguish between models that occasionally reach the correct answer and models that more consistently follow a plausible reasoning process. Overall, these results demonstrate the necessity of evaluating multimodal reasoning at the step level. Final-answer accuracy only indicates whether a model reaches the correct endpoint, but does not reveal whether the model follows a coherent cross-modal solution path. In contrast, our step-level evaluation scores provide a finer-grained diagnos-tic signal for assessing the quality of intermediate reasoning trajectories. This is particularly impor-tant for challenging multimodal STEM problems, where success depends not only on the final predic-tion, but also on whether the model can sustain a logically consistent reasoning chain across textual and visual steps. 

4.3.2 Analysis of Multiple Ground-Truth Solutions 

For problems with multiple valid solution strate-gies, we compute the process score against each ground-truth solution and examine the resulting distribution. As shown by the red intervals in Figure 4, the same model trajectory can receive substantially different scores under different refer-ences. This variation does not necessarily indicate unstable model behavior; rather, it reflects the fact that complex multimodal STEM problems often ad-mit multiple legitimate reasoning paths. A model may therefore follow a correct and coherent so-lution strategy while still obtaining a low score against one particular annotated reference. This observation highlights a limitation of single-reference process evaluation. When only one ground-truth solution is available, the metric may unfairly penalize trajectories that are valid but struc-turally different from the chosen annotation. To mitigate this issue, we provide multiple ground-truth solutions whenever alternative solving strate-gies exist. With this design, a model can obtain a high process score as long as its reasoning is close to at least one valid reference path. This improves both the stability and the fairness of the evaluation framework by reducing spurious low scores caused by annotation incompleteness. Moreover, the gap between the best-matched and worst-matched references is itself informative. A larger gap suggests greater diversity in valid solu-tion trajectories and indicates that multi-reference evaluation is particularly important for such prob-lems. Overall, these results show that multiple ground-truth solutions are essential for reliable step-level evaluation of multimodal reasoning. 4.3.3 Case Study 

Figure 5 presents a representative example involv-ing a two-mass system rotating about point A,where the goal is to derive the angular-momentum vector in the i1-j1-k1 basis. This problem admits two valid ground-truth solution trajectories with the same final answer. The first solution follows a direct vector-based derivation by explicitly comput-ing r × (Ω × r) for each mass, while the second so-lution reformulates the problem through the inertia tensor and derives H via IA(Ω k1). This example highlights a key challenge of step-level trajectory evaluation: even when two reasoning chains are both correct, they may differ substantially in their intermediate structure. Our framework correctly recognizes such alter-native reasoning paths. GPT-5.4 + GPT-Image is matched to Solution 1 and obtains a matched-solution score of 0.6453, together with a perfect final-answer score. By contrast, Gemini 3.1 Pro is matched to Solution 2 with a matched-solution score of 0.7009, again producing the correct final answer. Importantly, the two models are not re-quired to mimic the same sequence of intermediate steps. Instead, each model is rewarded because its reasoning remains semantically close to at least one valid reference trajectory. This result demonstrates that the proposed evaluation framework is able to accommodate diverse but valid solution strategies, rather than overfitting to a single annotated chain of thought. The example also illustrates the diagnostic value of step-level evaluation when the final prediction is incorrect. Qwen3.5-122B receives a relatively high score on problem understanding of 0.7271, sug-gesting that it correctly identifies the task setting and the relevant physical quantities. It also shows partial alignment with Solution 2 during interme-diate reasoning. However, the model ultimately produces an incorrect final expression and there-fore receives a zero score on the final-answer step, resulting in a substantially lower matched-solution score of 0.3635. A closer inspection suggests that the model captures part of the desired decomposi-tion, but fails to recover the complete k1 component in the final angular-momentum vector. Such fine-grained failure patterns would be invisible under final-answer accuracy alone. Overall, this case study demonstrates two im-portant properties of our benchmark. First, multi-reference annotation is necessary for fair process evaluation, since a single reference would incor-rectly penalize valid reasoning paths that differ in form but not in substance. Second, step-level scor-ing provides a more informative diagnosis of model behavior by revealing whether an error arises from misunderstanding the problem, from a flawed inter-mediate derivation, or only at the final composition stage. These observations further support the need for fine-grained, multi-reference evaluation in mul-timodal STEM reasoning. 

5 Conclusions 

We introduced S TEP STEM, a benchmark and evaluation framework for interleaved multimodal STEM reasoning. By combining expert-curated problems, step-level textual and visual annotations, and multi-solution trajectory matching, STEP -STEM enables evaluation of not only whether a model reaches the correct final answer, but also how it reasons across modalities. Experiments show that current state-of-the-art MLLMs still exhibit a substantial gap between answer correctness and rea-soning trajectories quality: to-text MLLMs cannot generate vision intermediate steps, whereas unified generative models better expose multimodal reason-ing behavior but remain far from reliable. We hope STEP STEM will support future research on multi-modal process supervision, reasoning-oriented data construction, and more faithful evaluation of cross-modal reasoning systems.

Appendix A Implementation Details 

To ensure complete reproducibility of our evalua-tion pipeline, we provide the detail of the hyperpa-rameters and configuration settings used through-out the experiments. 

A.1 Model Inference Configuration 

All baseline models are evaluated under a strict zero-shot setting. The generation temperature is set to 0.2 with a top-p of 0.9, which approximates greedy decoding, and the maximum generation limit is capped at 32 k tokens. We apply a unified system prompt enforcing a structured, interleaved output format. 

A.2 Local Evaluator Configuration 

For textual step alignment, we deploy a local Qwen3.5-9B model and aggregate the attention weights. The temperature parameter τ for attention normalization is set to 0.3. For image step align-ment, we utilize a pre-trained ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016) encoder. The diversity penalty hyperparame-ter λ is set to 0.2.

A.3 Global Judge and Metric Fusion 

The LLM-based global judge for assessing whole-trace coverage is also powered by Qwen3.5-9B. To ensure stable and deterministic judgments, we con-figure it with a temperature of 0.0, a top-p of 1.0,and a maximum token length of 512 . In the calcu-lation of the fused process score Sfused , the Local and Global scores are assigned equal importance (ws = 0 .5 and wj = 0 .5). 

B Baseline MLLMs 

B.1 Closed-source Unified Models GPT-5.4 + GPT-Image (OpenAI, 2026; Ope-nAI) A pipeline approach that explicitly couples OpenAI’s advanced large language model (GPT-5.4) with its image generation counterpart (GPT-Image) to handle complex interleaved text-and-image reasoning tasks. 

Gemini 2.5 Flash Image (Google DeepMind, 2025) A leading commercial multimodal model from Google designed with native capabilities to seamlessly process and generate both textual and visual outputs within a unified inference process. 

B.2 Open-Source Unified / Interleaved Generation Models Janus-Pro (Chen et al., 2025) An advanced open-source model designed for unified multi-modal understanding and generation, operating over both visual and textual modalities in a sin-gle framework. 

OmniGen2 (Wu et al., 2025a) An open-source multimodal model that integrates a diffusion-based image decoder with LLM hidden states, enabling context-driven image generation and visual reason-ing without compromising the model’s core textual capabilities. 

Showo2 (Xie et al., 2025) A native unified multi-modal model that innovatively fuses autoregressive modeling (for text and understanding tasks) and flow matching/diffusion modeling (for visual gen-eration) within a single Transformer architecture. 

Bagel (Deng et al., 2025) An open-source foun-dation model featuring a Mixture-of-Transformer-Experts (MoT) architecture. It dedicates separate expert networks to understanding and generation tasks while sharing a unified self-attention mecha-nism to efficiently handle interleaved text and im-age data. 

B.3 Understanding-only Models Claude Opus 4.6 (Anthropic, 2026) Anthropic’s flagship model known for exceptional logical rea-soning and visual understanding capabilities; how-ever, it operates strictly within a vision-text-to-text paradigm. 

Gemini 3.1 Pro (Google DeepMind, 2026) Google’s advanced multimodal understanding model. It excels at processing complex multimodal inputs to produce detailed textual reasoning traces but cannot explicitly generate visual evidence. 

Qwen Family (Qwen3.5-122B, Qwen3.5-Plus, Qwen3-VL-235B) (Qwen Team, 2026; Bai et al., 2025) A powerful series of models developed by the Qwen team. While possessing strong foun-dational language and vision-language understand-ing, they are fundamentally constrained to text-only generation and rely purely on textual or symbolic reasoning.
