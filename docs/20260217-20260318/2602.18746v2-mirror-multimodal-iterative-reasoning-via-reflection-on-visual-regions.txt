## Abstract 

In the era of Vision-Language Models (VLMs), enhancing multimodal reasoning capabilities re-mains a critical challenge, particularly in handling ambiguous or complex visual inputs, where initial inferences often lead to hallucinations or logic er-rors. Existing VLMs often produce plausible yet ungrounded answers, and even when prompted to “reflect”, their corrections may remain detached from the image evidence. To address this, we pro-pose the MIRROR framework for m ultimodal iterative r easoning via reflection on visual regions. By embedding visual reflection as a core mech-anism, MIRROR is formulated as a closed-loop process comprising draft, critique, region-based verification, and revision, which are repeated un-til the output is visually grounded. To facilitate training of this model, we construct ReflectV ,a visual reflective dataset for multi-turn supervi-sion that explicitly contains reflection triggers, region-based verification actions, and answer re-vision grounded in visual evidence. Experiments on both general vision-language benchmarks and representative vision-language reasoning bench-marks show that MIRROR improves correctness and reduces visual hallucinations, demonstrating the value of training reflection as an evidence-seeking, region-aware verification process rather than a purely textual revision step.

## 2. Related Work 

2.1. Vision-Language Models 

Recent years have witnessed rapid progress of Vision-Language Models (VLMs), which connect a powerful vi-sual encoder with a large language model (LLM) through lightweight adapters or cross-attention modules. Beyond generic image understanding, grounding and localization capabilities are increasingly integrated into VLMs to reduce hallucinations and support fine-grained visual reference. For example, LLaVA-NeXT (Liu et al., 2024a) leverages a dy-2MIRROR: Multimodal Iterative Reasoning via Reflection On Visual Regions 

namic high-resolution strategy to preserve subtle visual cues, while Kosmos-2 (Peng et al., 2023) introduces a tokenization to represent grounded text spans linked to bounding boxes, allowing models to generate text grounded in image re-gions. Recent open-source VLMs such as Qwen2.5-VL (Bai et al., 2025) and InternVL3 (Zhu et al., 2025) scale training data and refine alignment pipelines to improve instruction following, localization, OCR, and general visual-language competence. Regarding closed-source models, Gemini-3.0 Pro (Google, 2025) and GPT-5.2 (OpenAI, 2025) demon-strate strong general multimodal capabilities and inspire new reasoning patterns, motivating research on grounding and visual feedback. Building on the instruction-tuned VLM paradigm, our approach targets a more structured capabil-ity: enabling the model to execute a closed-loop reasoning process with iterative visual reflection. 

2.2. Multimodal Reasoning in VLMs 

The paradigm of multimodal reasoning in VLMs has pro-gressed significantly, evolving from standard instruction following to incorporating Chain-of-Thought (CoT) mecha-nisms (Wei et al., 2022) that align logical generation with vi-sual context, e.g., LLaVA-CoT (Xu et al., 2025). To further enhance robustness and mitigate hallucinations, recent ap-proaches have introduced self-correction strategies. For in-stance, Critique Fine-Tuning (Wang et al., 2025d) integrates explicit critique steps into the training objective, while meth-ods like FIRE (Li et al., 2024c) employ “response-feedback-refinement” triplets to enable models to iteratively refine answers based on feedback signals. In parallel, research in visual grounding has employed visual prompts—such as bounding boxes and points—to enhance referential pre-cision. Models like Shikra (Chen et al., 2023) and SoM-GPT4V (Yang et al., 2023) demonstrate that explicit visual markers can significantly aid in parsing fine-grained details. Beyond static visual grounding, recent work has begun to explore a broader “Thinking with Images” paradigm (Su et al., 2025b;a; Zhang et al., 2025a), where VLMs actively attend to visual evidence during reasoning to acquire finer-grained visual signals for verification. Nevertheless, prior works lack a reflection-driven control signal, while visual tool usage is rarely leveraged to rectify errors and enable targeted region verification. 

2.3. Reflection in Vision-language Models 

Reflection aims to address core issues in VLMs, such as inadequate reasoning rigor, insufficient utilization of visual information, and difficulty in self-correcting errors. Rel-evant studies have explored solutions through optimizing reflection mechanisms, enhancing visual interaction, and integrating external knowledge. SRPO (Wan et al., 2025) introduces the ⟨reflection ⟩ token and GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) to strengthen the model’s self-reflection capability. VL-Rethinker (Wang et al., 2025a) adopts selective sam-ple replay for effective data filtering and forced rethinking strategies, alleviating the diminishing reflection advantage. Look-Back (Yang et al., 2025) designs ⟨back ⟩ token to guide the model to revisit image information. Reflection-V (Jian et al., 2025) leverages a cold-start supervised phase to ex-tract visual details and then employs reinforcement learning with attention-aware rewards to incentivize the model’s fo-cus on critical visual information. Additionally, some other studies attempt to improve reasoning performance through multi-agent collaboration (e.g., Mulberry (Yao et al., 2024)) or tool-chain expansion (e.g., Thyme (Zhang et al., 2025b)). However, most existing approaches limit reflection to tex-tual reflections or implicit representations, often failing to actively revisit visual evidence. In contrast, the proposed 

MIRROR enables explicit visual reflection . By integrat-ing visual tools, our framework transforms reflection from a static revision step into an active verification process, where the model explicitly queries and checks specific image re-gions to ensure every correction is visually grounded. 

## 3. Method 

We propose MIRROR as shown in Figure 2, a visual re-flective framework that transforms one-pass VLM inference into a closed-loop verification process: (i) draft an initial an-swer; (ii) self-reflection to identify uncertainty or potential errors; (iii) visual verification by invoking a visual prompt generator to mark task-relevant regions; and (iv) refinement based on the updated visual evidence. 

3.1. Formulation 

We formulate the visual reflective reasoning task as a multi-turn generation process. Given an initial image I0 and a user query q, the objective of the VLM, denoted as πθ , is to gener-ate a structured reasoning trajectory Y = {y1, y 2, . . . , y K }

over K interaction turns. Unlike standard CoT reasoning, which operates on static visual features, our framework in-corporates dynamic visual interactions. At each step k, the model generates an output tuple yk = ( ak, r k, v k), where 

ak is the textual response, rk denotes the reflection content, and vk signifies the visual tool tokens for visual reflection. The tool tokens vk trigger a visual update function G, yield-ing an updated visual context Ik = G(I0, v k) that highlights specific regions for the subsequent step. 3.2. Tool-Augmented Visual Prompt Generation 

A key innovation of MIRROR is the integration of the visual prompt generator G for visual reflection. This module allows the model to actively “verify” its reflection by grounding textual hypotheses into crucial visual signals. The workflow proceeds as follows: 

Triggering the Tool: When the model identifies some con-tent in its reasoning chain for further verification, it initiates a request enclosed within a special token structure vk:

<tool call> {

name : "Visual Prompt Generator" ,

flag : true/false anchor : "keywords..." args : {color : "red" , shape : "point" }}</tool call> 

(2) where name invokes the visual module, anchor specifies the textual target derived from reflection for localization, and 

args defines the rendering attributes for visual prompts. 

Visual Reflection: Upon receiving vk, the generator G ex-ecutes a two-stage pipeline. First, it employs Molmo-7B (Deitke et al., 2025) to ground the text anchor into point coordinates. Second, these coordinates are used as prompts for SAM 2 (Ravi et al., 2024) to generate precise visual markers (e.g., masks or bounding boxes) overlaid on the original image, and we obtain a new image Ik.

Reasoning Refinement: The resulting image Ik, now ex-plicitly highlighting the neglected or misinterpreted regions, is fed back into the VLM. This closes the loop, forcing the model to re-attend to the visual evidence ( Ik) alongside the query history before generating the next reasoning step.

4.2. Dialogue Filtering Strategy 

To guarantee the instructional quality of the reflective tra-jectories, we enforce a rigorous filtering protocol based on the evaluation score st assigned to each turn t. A dia-logue trajectory is preserved only if it strictly adheres to the following criteria. Steady improvement: The scores must exhibit a strictly ascending trend ( st+1 > s t), ensur-ing that the model demonstrates a valid pathway of error correction rather than fluctuating reasoning; Successful con-vergence: The dialogue must conclude with a perfect score (sfinal = 10 ), indicating that the reflection successfully re-solved the initial error; Ground Truth alignment: Beyond the teacher’s score, the final student response must be semanti-cally consistent with the dataset’s ground truth, preventing false positives where the verifier might be misled. 

4.3. Visual Grounding via Keyword Extraction 

To prevent reflection from degenerating into text-only spec-ulation, we explicitly ground the introspective process with task-adaptive keywords that are later used as tool arguments for visual prompting (Section 3.2). Specifically, for general VQA, we parse entity-centric keywords directly from the reflection. In contrast, for dense perception tasks (e.g., OCR, ChartQ) where reflection lacks spatial specificity, we derive precise keywords from ground truth annotations. To ensure strict alignment between textual thought and visual evidence, we inject visual attribute descriptions (e.g., “as indicated by the red point” ) back into the reflection text. This visually grounded text is then paired with the marker-overlaid image to constitute a composite input for the subsequent iteration. 

4.4. Self-Reflective Conversion Mechanism 

To endow our model with self-correction capabilities, we propose a Self-reflective Conversion mechanism. Specifi-cally, we prompt Qwen2.5-7B (Yang et al., 2024) to rewrite the Teacher’s corrective feedback (e.g., “No, your answer is incorrect...” ) into a first-person reflective thought process (e.g., “Wait, upon closer inspection, I realize my previous answer was incorrect...” ). This effectively transforms exter-nal criticism into self-reflection, teaching the model how to diagnose its own errors. 

4.5. Visual Verification and Trajectory Adaptation 

To ensure precise visual grounding and maintain a balanced training distribution, we implement a dual-stage pipeline after being processed by the visual tool: 

Visual Consistency Filtering. We employ a verifier, Qwen2.5-VL-7B (Bai et al., 2025) to assess the semantic consistency between the generated visual prompts and texts. This step ensures that the generated marks accurately align with the entities described in the corrective feedback, filter-ing out instances of ungrounded or ambiguous visualization.

## 5. Experiment 

To comprehensively assess the performance of our model, we utilize the VLMEvalKit (Duan et al., 2024) framework, a standardized toolkit for evaluating VLMs. We employ GPT-4o-mini (Hurst et al., 2024) as the judge to evaluate the correctness of the model’s responses if needed for some specific benchmarks. We conduct a comprehensive evalu-ation across diverse benchmarks tailored to specific multi-modal capabilities. For General Capabilities, we employ MM-Vet (Yu et al., 2023), MMStar (Chen et al., 2024), and SeedBench2-Plus (Li et al., 2024a). In the domain of OCR & Document Understanding, we utilize TextVQA (Singh et al., 2019), OCRBench (Liu et al., 2024b), and ChartQA (Masry et al., 2022). To assess Hallucination and robust-ness, we include POPE (Li et al., 2023) and HallusionBench (Guan et al., 2024). Furthermore, we evaluate Fine-grained Perception using HRBench (Wang et al., 2025c), MME-RealWorld-Lite (Zhang et al., 2024), and VStarBench (Wu & Xie, 2023), alongside MathVision (Wang et al., 2024) for mathematical reasoning tasks. For further details on the dataset and experimental setup, including discussions on data quality and the effectiveness of trajectory adaptation, please refer to Appendix Sections A and B.

## 6. Conclusion 

In this work, we have presented MIRROR , a framework that enhances multimodal reasoning by transforming reflec-tion from a purely textual revision into an active, closed-loop visual verification process. To facilitate this, we con-struct ReflectV , a high-quality dataset designed to teach models to explicitly ground their reasoning in specific im-age regions. Our experiments demonstrate that MIRROR significantly outperforms strong baselines, validating that equipping VLMs with the agency to “look again” and verify visual evidence is essential for reducing hallucinations and achieving robust reasoning. Despite its effectiveness, our approach currently faces limi-tations in abstract domains and coarse-grained attribute

binding , where spatial anchoring is inherently difficult. We provide a detailed analysis of these constraints in Appendix Section E. Future work will focus on enhancing the granu-larity of the verification process and exploring mechanisms to anchor symbolic reasoning within visual contexts. 

## Impact Statement 

This paper presents work aimed at advancing Vision-Language Models by enhancing their reasoning capabili-ties and mitigating hallucinations. By enabling models to verify their outputs against visual evidence, our framework, MIRROR, contributes to the development of more reliable, grounded, and trustworthy AI systems. While all AI ad-vancements carry potential societal implications, this work primarily focuses on improving technical robustness and correctness, and we do not foresee any specific negative societal consequences that must be highlighted here.

## B. Implementation Details 

B.1. Training Details. 

We utilize LLaMA-Factory (Zheng et al., 2024) to perform Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on the Qwen2.5-VL-7B (Bai et al., 2025). The training is conducted on 2 × NVIDIA H100 GPUs for 3.0 epochs. To ensure training efficiency and memory optimization, we utilize the DeepSpeed ZeRO-3 optimization and employ Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) for parameter-efficient fine-tuning. The LoRA rank is set to r = 32 with the scaling factor α = 128 .

B.2. Inference Details. 

We perform all evaluations using the SFT-adapted MIRROR model. And we use the same hardware settings in all experiments. To ensure high-quality visual grounding, the visual prompt generator is powered by Molmo-7B (Deitke et al., 2025), which predicts precise point coordinates based on the model’s textual reflection. For mask generation, we employ the SAM 2.1 (Ravi et al., 2024) model, specifically loading the large-scale checkpoint sam2.1 hiera l.pt to ensure robust segmentation performance even in complex visual scenes. 

B.3. Trajectory Length Analysis. 

To understand the model’s dynamic behavior, we conducted a statistical analysis of the inference trajectories across evaluated benchmarks, as illustrated in Figure 8. We observe that the model adaptively adjusts its reasoning depth based on the inherent difficulty of the task: • Efficiency in Information Extraction: For straightforward queries where visual evidence is explicit, the model tends to converge efficiently. For instance, on ChartQA, which primarily involves direct value extraction, 98.7% of samples are resolved within a single round, minimizing unnecessary computational overhead. • Rigor in Complex Perception: In contrast, for tasks requiring fine-grained verification or hallucination mitigation, the trajectory extends significantly. On MME-RealWorld and HallusionBench, the model engages in a second round of visual reflection for 58.6% and 57.0% of cases, respectively. Notably, MathVision exhibits the highest proportion of trajectories exceeding three rounds (2.5%), reflecting the need for iterative correction in multi-step logical derivation. Overall, with 80.2% of total samples resolved in the first turn, this distribution confirms that MIRROR effectively balances efficiency and rigor, engaging in deep visual reflection only when the complexity of the query demands it. ## C. Prompt 

C.1. Prompt for Dataset Construction 

To construct the training data for the ReflectV dataset, we employ a multi-stage prompting strategy to transform raw annotations into grounded, first-person reflective trajectories. First, we generate task-specific visual cues. For data-intensive tasks like Charts, OCR, and Doc, we utilize a specific prompt to extract key subjects and entities as shown in Figure 9. For general QA tasks, we employ a two-step process: in Figure 10, first identifying key physical objects mentioned in the feedback, and subsequently merging them into a concise caption. ## E. Limitation 

To provide a comprehensive understanding of MIRROR’s boundaries, we present qualitative examples of failure cases in the figures below. These cases correspond to the two primary limitations discussed in the main paper: the difficulty in grounding abstract concepts and the lack of granularity in complex attribute binding. 

1. Limited Applicability in Abstract Domains. Despite these improvements, MIRROR still faces challenges in symbolic derivation involving complex spatial mapping. Figure 21 illustrates a geometry problem requiring the calculation of a circle’s radius using the Pythagorean theorem. While the model correctly performs the symbolic derivation in the text, its attempt to invoke the visual prompt generator is ineffective. The model tries to ground the concept of “diameter calculation and radius division” into visual points. However, mathematical operations and logical variables are abstract concepts that lack a direct spatial mapping in the 2D image plane. Consequently, the generated visual cues (red points) provide no informative feedback for verifying the correctness of the arithmetic steps. 

2. Coarse-grained Attribute Binding. Furthermore, MIRROR encounters difficulties in fine-grained attribute binding under compositional constraints.
